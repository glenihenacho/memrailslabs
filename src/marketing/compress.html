L1 through L4 narrow. L5 synthesizes.

The first four layers do progressively more expensive work on a progressively smaller candidate set: a literal scan throws away most files, key lookup throws away most rows, semantic rank throws away most claims, evidence filtering throws away most candidates. What survives is ~5–15 high-confidence pieces of evidence with citations, contradictions surfaced, confidence calibrated.

L5 is where that surviving material becomes something useful. Without L5, the agent receives a list of fragments and has to do its own synthesis — which means burning context window on raw evidence, re-deriving relevance, and producing inconsistent compressions across calls. With L5, the agent receives the packet: a structured, deterministic compression with provenance preserved.

The packet is the product. The model that produces it is ours.

Three problems with passthrough

Why we don't route your packets through someone else.

The obvious build would have been to take retrieved evidence and POST it to a frontier model for compression. We considered it. Three specific problems made it untenable for the long product — each independently disqualifying.

01

The model doesn't know the shape of memory.

A general LLM trained on the open internet has no prior on claim/evidence/confidence frontmatter, no instinct for contradiction surfacing, no calibrated behavior around citation density. It compresses adequately and inconsistently. We needed a model that knows what a packet should be — and that means fine-tuning on packets.

Result: a 4–7 point quality gap on internal packet-fidelity evals against frontier passthrough.
02

Per-token bills compound badly.

At 10K queries/month, passthrough costs almost nothing. At 1M queries/month, it becomes the structural margin problem — a per-call vendor bill that scales linearly with revenue and never amortizes. Owning the inference flips the curve: high fixed cost at low volume, near-zero marginal cost at scale. The bigger we get, the cheaper each packet gets.

Result: passthrough margin caps at ~30–45%; owned inference reaches ~85%+ at Business scale.
03

The moat evaporates the day they ship an API.

If the compression is a generic LLM call, any competitor can wire up the same endpoint and ship a packet that looks similar enough. The differentiation has to live inside the model — fine-tuned on data we collect, on tasks we define, with failure modes we own. A passthrough doesn't compound; a fine-tune does.

Result: every query MemRails serves is a training signal for the next checkpoint.

The economics

A fixed-cost layer that gets cheaper per packet at scale.

A dedicated GPU costs the same whether it serves ten thousand packets a month or a million. Owning the inference means paying a fixed floor up front — and watching the per-packet cost collapse as volume arrives. This is exactly the cost-curve a passthrough vendor cannot match.

The flywheel

Why the moat compounds.

Owning the model is not the moat. Owning the model that gets better every week from the network's own traffic is the moat. Every query is a training signal; every refactor is a quality label; every dispute is a hard negative. Compounding works in one direction.

Compounding in one direction

A passthrough vendor sees your traffic but has no incentive to optimize for your task. A fine-tune that uses your traffic improves at your task every cycle. The same data flow that pays our infrastructure bill also trains the next checkpoint.

Weekly cadence, observable rollouts

A new checkpoint ships every Monday after passing the regression suite. Canary deploys for 24 hours; rollback window 90 seconds; eval scores published in the changelog. You can audit which version produced any packet by its model_version field.

The asymmetry

A competitor copying the API can match the contract. They cannot match the corpus. Two and a half million graded query/packet pairs accrued over twelve months is not a weekend project — and ours grows weekly. Time is on the model's side.
